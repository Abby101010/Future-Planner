# Creative Projects

Methodologies for projects that produce a deliverable artifact — a book, an app, a film, a product launch. The central tension: creative work resists deadlines, but without deadlines it resists completion.

## Phase Gates

Creative projects have natural phases; moving forward before the current phase is solid causes rework. Gate transitions explicitly.

1. **Concept** — the idea, the audience, the core promise. Gate: can you summarize the project in two sentences, and does someone not-you find it interesting?
2. **Design / outline** — the structure. Gate: a table of contents, storyboard, wireframes, or spec exists and is internally consistent.
3. **Build / draft** — the first pass, ugly is fine. Gate: an end-to-end artifact exists even if every part is weak.
4. **Revise** — iterate toward quality. Gate: external feedback captured from 3+ people in the target audience.
5. **Polish** — final pass for surface details. Gate: no known blockers; only taste-level decisions remain.
6. **Ship** — release. Gate: it's actually out in the world.

Don't polish in the draft phase. Don't add features in the polish phase.

## MVP Thinking for Creative Work

"Minimum viable" doesn't mean low quality — it means the smallest complete version that exercises the entire idea. A short story before a novel. A 5-minute short before a feature. A landing page before a product.

The first full artifact teaches you more than six months of refining part of it.

## Dependency Mapping

Creative projects have technical dependencies (build system, asset pipeline, printer, venue) and creative dependencies (can't write chapter 4 until chapter 2's voice is settled). Map them:

- **Technical** — resolvable with time/money. Do these early to clear the critical path.
- **Creative** — resolvable only with insight. Don't force a resolution; gather material and return.

A blocked creative dependency is a signal to switch to a parallel track (a different chapter, a different scene), not to push through.

## The Shitty First Draft

Anne Lamott's principle: permission to write badly is the mechanism that produces anything at all. The first pass exists to give you something to revise. Perfectionism during drafting is the most common reason projects stall.

## Launch Mechanics

- **Internal launch before external launch** — show it to 5 trusted people before 500 strangers. Fix the obvious 10 things.
- **Have a launch date** — without one, the project drifts. Work expands to fill available time.
- **Launch the smallest version** — iterate in public once it's out.

## Clarification questions a goal-clarifier should ask for creative projects

- What's the final artifact and format? How will someone consume it?
- Who's the audience? What will they do with it?
- Do you have a deadline, and is it self-imposed or external?
- Which phase are you in right now? What's the current blocker?
- Do you have a collaborator, an editor, or critique partners?
- What's the minimum shipped version that would be satisfying?

## Common failure modes

- **Endless concept phase** — polishing the pitch without starting the work. Set a hard date to exit concept.
- **Perfection in the draft** — rewriting chapter 1 for the 12th time while chapters 4–10 don't exist. Draft linearly to the end, then revise.
- **No audience** — building for an imagined audience that doesn't exist. Show work early to real humans.
- **Scope creep** — each revision adds a new feature/plot/scene. Freeze scope when entering polish.
