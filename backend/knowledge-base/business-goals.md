# Business Goals

Methodologies for launching, growing, or exiting a business or product. The failure mode is working on the wrong thing confidently; the antidote is short learning cycles.

## OKRs (Doerr / Grove)

Objectives + Key Results separate direction from measurement.

- **Objective** — qualitative, directional, inspiring. "Become the default tool for X audience."
- **Key Results** — 3–5 quantitative measures that prove the objective is happening. "10,000 monthly active users." "30% week-4 retention." "$50k MRR."

Properties of good KRs: concrete, time-bound, outside-observable. If two people could disagree on whether the KR was hit, it's not a good KR.

## Lean Startup (Ries)

The central unit of progress is the **validated learning cycle**:

1. **Build** — the smallest experiment that could test a specific belief.
2. **Measure** — one metric that distinguishes success from failure before you look.
3. **Learn** — update the underlying business hypothesis.

A pivot is a change in one of the hypotheses (customer, problem, solution, channel) while holding the others steady. Random thrashing is not a pivot.

## Traction Metrics that Actually Matter

Early-stage businesses drown in vanity metrics. A small number of signals tell you whether something is working:

- **Week-4 retention** — how many people who signed up a month ago are still active? Below ~20% and you don't have a product.
- **Organic growth rate** — what % of new users come without paid acquisition? Organic growth is the signal of real fit.
- **Direct revenue from target customer** — not friends-and-family, not "pilot" goodwill, real willingness to pay from the named ICP.
- **NPS from active users** — qualitative, but brutally informative at small scale.

## Time-Boxing Experiments

Each experiment needs a **kill criterion** written down before it starts. "If by week 6 we don't see X, we stop this and pivot." Without kill criteria, founders cling to dying experiments out of sunk cost.

## The Customer Development Loop (Blank)

Talk to customers at every phase. Specifically:

1. **Problem interviews** — before building. Confirm the pain exists and matters.
2. **Solution interviews** — mockups, prototypes. Confirm your solution clicks.
3. **Usage interviews** — after first usage. Confirm the value landed.

Rule: never do a "demo" when you should do an interview. Ask open questions, then shut up.

## Clarification questions a goal-clarifier should ask for business goals

- What's the business or product, and who's the target customer (specifically, not "SMBs")?
- Stage: pre-idea, pre-revenue, post-revenue pre-PMF, post-PMF scaling?
- What's the top-line KR you want to hit by when?
- What's the current bottleneck — can't find customers, can't build fast enough, can't keep them, can't make money?
- How are you funding it — salary, savings, investment, side project?
- What's the kill criterion if it isn't working?

## Common failure modes

- **Building before validating** — months of engineering on a problem nobody has.
- **Vanity metrics** — total signups, press mentions, social followers. Pretty, not predictive.
- **No kill criterion** — experiments run indefinitely because stopping feels like failure.
- **Avoiding customer contact** — founders who haven't talked to 20 target customers are working on a fiction.
- **Chasing every idea** — focus is the rare and expensive thing. Write down what you're NOT doing.
