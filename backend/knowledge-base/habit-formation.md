# Habit Formation

Methodologies for building behaviors that persist without willpower. A habit is a behavior that has become its own cue — thinking about doing it is optional.

## The Habit Loop (Duhigg / Wood)

Every habit has three parts:

1. **Cue** — a stable trigger in the environment. Time, location, preceding action, emotional state.
2. **Routine** — the behavior itself.
3. **Reward** — the felt consequence that closes the loop.

To build a habit, design the cue explicitly. To break one, change the cue or the routine — the reward is harder to alter.

## Habit Stacking (Fogg / Clear)

The easiest cue is an action you already do reliably. Anchor new behavior to a stable existing one:

- "After I pour my morning coffee, I read one page."
- "After I brush my teeth at night, I write one sentence in my journal."
- "After I close my laptop for the day, I do 10 pushups."

The template is: *"After [existing habit], I will [new behavior]."* Keep the new behavior absurdly small at first — the point is consistency, not volume.

## Minimum Viable Habit

The first version of a habit should be so small it feels almost embarrassing. Not "30 minutes of meditation" but "sit down on the cushion for 60 seconds." Not "write 1000 words" but "open the document and write one sentence."

Why: consistency compounds; volume does not. 60 seconds every day beats 30 minutes three times in a row followed by giving up.

Scale up only after the tiny version is fully automatic (typically 2–6 weeks).

## The Two-Day Rule

Never miss twice in a row. Missing once is noise; missing twice is the start of extinction. This rule prevents the all-or-nothing collapse where one broken day spirals into quitting.

## Identity-Based Habits

"I am becoming someone who..." works better long-term than "I will..." goals. Each rep of the habit is a vote for the identity. A goal like "write a book" is a single finish line; the identity "I am a writer" is self-renewing.

## Environment Design

Willpower is a finite daily resource and unreliable. Environment is stable.

- Want to practice guitar? Leave it out on a stand, not in a case in a closet.
- Want to eat better? Don't keep the tempting food in the house.
- Want to run in the morning? Put the running clothes next to the bed.

Friction is the dominant variable. Each layer of friction between cue and routine roughly halves the probability of the behavior.

## Clarification questions a goal-clarifier should ask for habit goals

- What specific behavior, at what frequency, for what duration?
- What existing daily routine could this stack onto?
- What's tripped you up on similar habits before — missed days, life disruption, boredom?
- Is there a social or accountability component (partner, group, public commitment)?
- What's the smallest possible version that would still count as doing it?
- What's the reward or positive signal at the end?

## Common failure modes

- **Too ambitious on day 1** — inconsistent beats intense. Start laughably small.
- **No explicit cue** — relying on "remembering" fails. Attach to a specific existing behavior.
- **All-or-nothing mindset** — one missed day triggers abandonment. Adopt the two-day rule.
- **Measuring volume instead of streak** — for habits, consistency is the metric. For performance, volume comes later.
