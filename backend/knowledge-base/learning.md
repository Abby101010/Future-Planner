# Learning

Methodologies for acquiring skills, knowledge, and fluency. Learning is slow by nature; the goal is to remove friction, not to accelerate past what the brain can consolidate.

## Spaced Repetition

Forgetting is not a bug — it's the signal that tells you what to review. The forgetting curve (Ebbinghaus) shows recall decays exponentially unless re-activated.

- Review material just before you would forget it. Spacing grows with each successful recall: 1 day → 3 days → 1 week → 2 weeks → 1 month.
- Tools: Anki, Mochi, handwritten index cards. The tool matters less than the consistency of daily review.
- Quality of cards matters more than quantity. One card = one atomic fact, phrased as a question.

## Deliberate Practice (Ericsson)

Not all practice improves performance. Deliberate practice has four properties:

1. **Specific goal** — "reduce error rate on conditional sentences by half," not "study Spanish."
2. **Focused attention** — not while multitasking, not on autopilot.
3. **Immediate feedback** — a tutor, an answer key, a test harness. No feedback = no improvement.
4. **At the edge of ability** — uncomfortable, not impossible. If it's easy you're reinforcing, not growing.

90 minutes/day of deliberate practice beats 6 hours of unfocused exposure.

## Bloom's Taxonomy for Sequencing

Skills layer in order. Attempting creation before understanding produces frustration.

1. **Remember** — recall facts, terms, conventions.
2. **Understand** — explain concepts in your own words.
3. **Apply** — use the concept on a new, structured problem.
4. **Analyze** — break a problem apart, identify structure.
5. **Evaluate** — judge trade-offs between approaches.
6. **Create** — build something novel that expresses the concept.

When stuck, drop down one level. Someone stuck on "create novel programs" often hasn't solidified "apply the concept on new problems."

## The Feynman Technique

To test whether you understand something: explain it as you would to a smart 12-year-old. If you reach for jargon or get vague, the jargon is hiding a gap. Close the gap, then re-explain.

## Interleaving vs Blocking

- **Blocking** — practice one skill until mastered, then next. Feels efficient. Usually isn't for retention.
- **Interleaving** — rotate between related skills within a session. Slower initial gains, far better long-term retention and transfer.

For domains with discriminable but related concepts (math topics, language grammar points, chord progressions), interleave.

## Clarification questions a goal-clarifier should ask for learning goals

- What specific capability would count as "learned it"? Describe what you'd be able to do.
- Do you have a deadline (exam, conference, project)?
- What's your current baseline — true beginner, rusty intermediate, specialist branching?
- How many hours/week can you dedicate, realistically? When in the day are those hours?
- Is there a feedback mechanism in place (tutor, class, graded assignments)?
- Do you learn better solo, with a cohort, or with a coach?

## Common failure modes

- **Tutorial hell** — consuming content without producing anything. Set a rule: 1 hour of input → 1 hour of output (exercises, writing, code).
- **Collector's fallacy** — buying courses, books, decks without using them. Enough ≠ done.
- **No feedback loop** — studying without testing. You don't know what you don't know until you fail at it.
- **Switching too often** — each switch costs ramp-up. If changing domains, finish a visible chunk first.
