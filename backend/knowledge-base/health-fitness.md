# Health & Fitness

Methodologies for physical training, body composition, and long-term health. Sustainability compounds; heroic efforts rarely do.

## Progressive Overload

The body adapts to stress slightly above the current comfort zone. Training that doesn't progress plateaus, and training that progresses too fast injures.

- **Strength** — add weight, reps, or sets gradually. 2.5–5% increment per week on main lifts when form is stable.
- **Endurance** — 10% rule: don't increase weekly volume by more than ~10% over the previous week.
- **Skill / mobility** — progress by positional difficulty or range, not just volume.

Track one number per modality. Trend over 8–12 weeks matters; day-to-day noise doesn't.

## Recovery as a First-Class Variable

Adaptation happens during recovery, not during training. Under-recovered work accumulates as fatigue masquerading as training, then injury.

- **Sleep** — 7–9h is the range where most systems work. Sustained <6h compounds as immune, cognitive, and mood debt.
- **Deload** — every 4–6 weeks reduce volume 40–50% for one week. Non-negotiable for anyone training hard.
- **Nutrition** — protein floor (≥0.7–1 g per lb bodyweight for strength goals), adequate carbs around training, water.
- **Rest days** — not zero-movement days; easy walks and mobility count. Just not further training stress.

## Sustainability > Optimality

An 80-percent-ideal routine done consistently beats a 100-percent-ideal routine abandoned after three weeks. Optimize for *adherence* first:

- Prefer 4 realistic workouts to 6 aspirational ones.
- Prefer food you actually enjoy and can prepare to the theoretically optimal macro split.
- Prefer a gym 5 minutes away to the best gym 45 minutes away.

Friction kills the routine, not lack of motivation.

## Body Composition Reality

- Fat loss is dominated by calorie balance, not exercise choice.
- Muscle gain requires a mild calorie surplus and progressive resistance training; you cannot efficiently do both at once (beyond the first 6–12 months of training).
- Visible changes happen over months, not weeks. Monthly photos + a weekly morning-weight trend line beat daily weigh-ins.
- Expected rate: ~0.5–1% bodyweight/week for fat loss, ~0.5–2 lb/month lean gain past the novice phase.

## Injury Risk

The most common injury causes are: ramping too fast, poor sleep, ignored mobility deficits, training through pain. Pain is signal, not noise. If something hurts for more than 3 sessions, modify the movement or see a provider.

## Clarification questions a goal-clarifier should ask for health/fitness goals

- What specific outcome: strength number, run time, body composition, rehab milestone, or "feel better"?
- Current baseline — days/week training, longest streak in last year, any injuries active?
- Time available: realistic hours/week, not aspirational.
- Equipment / access — gym, home gym, bodyweight only, outdoor.
- Any medical considerations or ongoing pain?
- Is the goal timeline realistic? (fat loss ~0.5–1%/wk; muscle gain ~0.5–1 lb/month)

## Common failure modes

- **Heroic first two weeks** — 6 workouts a week, strict diet, then burnout. Ramp in over 4–8 weeks.
- **Skipping recovery** — more is not better. Deload weeks are training, not time off.
- **Chasing novelty** — changing routines every 2 weeks prevents any adaptation signal.
- **Optimization before consistency** — the perfect split doesn't matter if you're doing it 2×/week instead of 4.
- **Ignoring pain** — "pushing through" a mechanical injury trades weeks now for months later.
