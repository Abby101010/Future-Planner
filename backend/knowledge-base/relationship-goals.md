# Relationship Goals

Methodologies for interpersonal goals — deepening connections, repairing ruptures, building community, finding a partner. Relationships are the domain most resistant to engineering thinking; they reward attention, patience, and self-awareness.

## Communication Frameworks

### Nonviolent Communication (Rosenberg)

Observe → Feel → Need → Request, separated explicitly.

- **Observation** — what happened, without interpretation. "You didn't text me back for three days."
- **Feeling** — your internal state. "I felt anxious and a bit hurt."
- **Need** — the underlying human need. "I need consistency to feel secure in the relationship."
- **Request** — a specific, actionable ask. "Would you be willing to let me know if you're going quiet for a day or more?"

The mix-up most people make is collapsing observation with interpretation ("you ignored me") — this triggers defensiveness and loses the actual signal.

## Attachment Theory (Basics)

Attachment styles describe patterns in how people handle closeness and distance. They aren't destiny but they predict friction points.

- **Secure** — comfortable with closeness and autonomy.
- **Anxious** — sensitive to distance, fears abandonment, seeks reassurance.
- **Avoidant** — sensitive to loss of autonomy, retreats under pressure.
- **Disorganized** — both patterns activated, often from early trauma.

Anxious + avoidant is the classic conflict: one pursues, the other withdraws, each behavior confirms the other's fear. The fix isn't blame; it's learning to name what's happening during it.

## The Gottman Research

Decades of data on what predicts relationship success:

- **5:1 positive to negative interactions** — in the everyday, not in crisis moments. Small bids for connection matter more than grand gestures.
- **Bids for connection** — tiny attempts to engage: a comment, a touch, a share. Turning toward them (not away) predicts longevity.
- **The Four Horsemen** — predictors of breakdown: criticism, contempt, defensiveness, stonewalling. Contempt is the single strongest predictor.
- **Repair attempts** — the ability to de-escalate mid-conflict matters more than avoiding conflict.

## Building vs. Maintaining vs. Repairing

Different phases need different moves:

- **Building** — consistent small exposure, shared novel experiences, vulnerability that invites reciprocity.
- **Maintaining** — rituals (weekly dinner, monthly trip), explicit appreciation, protecting time from erosion by work.
- **Repairing** — acknowledging impact before explaining intent, actually changing behavior (not just apologizing), allowing the other person their timeline.

## The Vulnerability Gradient

Connection deepens when one person takes a small vulnerability risk and the other meets it with care, then the roles swap. Trying to shortcut this with premature intensity ("trauma dumping") often backfires. Small risks, met well, build the trust that supports larger ones.

## Clarification questions a goal-clarifier should ask for relationship goals

- What specifically would be different? A concrete behavior, not "better."
- Is this about one specific relationship, a category (friends, family, romantic), or building from scratch?
- What's the current state — close and growing, distant but cordial, in conflict, estranged?
- What's been tried? What didn't work and why do you think?
- What's your part in the pattern you're trying to change?
- What's the other person's willingness — enthusiastic, reluctant, unknown?

## Common failure modes

- **Trying to fix the other person** — the only lever you have is your own behavior.
- **Avoidance framed as patience** — hoping time alone will repair a rupture. It rarely does without a move.
- **Grand gestures without the daily 5:1** — one apology dinner doesn't offset months of small disconnections.
- **Collapsing observation with interpretation** — "you never listen" will never land. "You looked at your phone three times while I was telling that story" might.
- **Setting goals for other people's behavior** — you can invite, model, and hold boundaries. You cannot set another person's goals.
