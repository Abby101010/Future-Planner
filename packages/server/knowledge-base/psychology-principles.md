# Psychology Principles

Behavioural-science rules for advising on motivation, habit formation, setbacks, and recovery.

## Self-Determination Theory (SDT)

Intrinsic motivation is produced by three psychological needs being met:

- **Autonomy** — "I chose this." The user must experience the plan as self-endorsed, not imposed. Framing matters: *"here's what I recommend, does this fit?"* preserves autonomy. *"you need to do this"* erodes it.
- **Competence** — "I can do this." Tasks must stretch the user without overwhelming them. Competence signal comes from visible progress — if the plan produces nothing observable for weeks, competence starves.
- **Relatedness** — "this matters to someone, including me." Goals tied to stated identity ("I am becoming a writer") or to a concrete beneficiary ("my kids will see a healthier parent") outlast purely abstract targets.

When a user is losing motivation, diagnose which of the three is undernourished and address that specifically. Generic encouragement ("you got this!") usually targets none of them.

## The Habit Loop

From Duhigg, grounded in Graybiel's basal-ganglia research:

**Cue → Routine → Reward**

- **Cue**: specific, sensory, consistent — a time, a location, a preceding event. "After I pour my morning coffee" beats "in the morning."
- **Routine**: the behaviour itself, scaled small enough to be reliable on bad days. A habit must survive low-motivation days to compound.
- **Reward**: must be immediate and perceptible. Long-term outcomes ("I'll be fitter in six months") are not rewards — the brain does not wire habits on them. Immediate rewards: a visible streak, a satisfying check-off, a brief moment of pride, a small sensory treat.

When helping the user build a habit, make all three components explicit. When a habit is collapsing, the cue is usually the first failure point.

## Implementation Intentions

From Gollwitzer — a 2–3× effect size on follow-through. The formula:

**"When situation X arises, I will perform behaviour Y."**

Not *"I'll exercise more this week"* (a goal) but *"On Mon/Wed/Fri at 7am, as soon as I finish brushing my teeth, I will put on running shoes and walk out the door"* (an implementation intention).

Key elements: specific time, specific place, specific trigger cue, specific action. The plan must be executable on autopilot — no decisions required in the moment.

In the planner: when the user commits to a new habit, refuse to schedule it generically. Pin it to a trigger and a location.

## Small Wins & Momentum

Teresa Amabile's research on the "progress principle": the single strongest predictor of daily motivation at work is **making progress on meaningful work**. Not the size of the progress — the fact of it.

Implications:
- Start every plan with a high-confidence first task. Success on day 1 buys motivation for day 2.
- When a user has been stuck, shrink the next task until success is nearly certain. "Write for 10 minutes" produces more writing over a month than "write for 2 hours."
- Visible progress artefacts (completed tasks, filled streaks, word counts, line counts) matter more than private satisfaction.

## Fresh-Start Effect

From Dai, Milkman & Riis: behaviour change is substantially more likely to stick when initiated at a temporal landmark — Monday, the first of the month, a birthday, the start of a quarter, post-vacation.

Use these windows deliberately. When a user is contemplating a new habit mid-week, and the change is non-urgent, suggesting a Monday start is legitimate — not procrastination. The landmark increases follow-through.

## Recovery from Missed Days

The single biggest predictor of *long-term* habit success is not how consistent someone is, but **how fast they recover after a miss.** One missed day has minimal effect. Two in a row dramatically increases the chance of full abandonment.

Guidance for recovery mode:
- **Never pile on.** Do not suggest "make up yesterday's missed task today on top of today's task." That compounds the overload that caused the miss.
- **Shrink, don't skip.** Today's version of the task should be smaller than normal, not larger. "Got the run in, even if it was 10 minutes" preserves identity ("I am someone who runs") better than "rest day."
- **Name the pattern, not the character.** *"Two skipped days in a row — let's find what's making this hard"* is useful. *"You're falling off again"* is not.
- **Diagnose before adjusting.** Ask: was the plan too ambitious, the cue unreliable, or an external disruption (illness, travel, crisis)? The fix differs.

## Loss Aversion & Sunk Cost

Kahneman/Tversky: losses feel roughly 2× as painful as equivalent gains feel good. Consequences:

- **Streaks have outsized power** — the fear of breaking a 30-day streak pulls more behaviour than the promise of reaching a 60-day one. Use streaks, but counterbalance: a rigidly-enforced streak that punishes one miss can backfire when it breaks.
- **Sunk-cost traps**: users who have been pursuing a goal for months will resist admitting it is the wrong goal. When the evidence says so, name it gently and help them separate "I invested time" from "I should keep investing."

## Capacity & Cognitive Load

Willpower / executive function depletes across the day (ego depletion is contested in basic research but holds well in real-world planning contexts). Rules:

- Place high-self-control tasks (resisting urges, writing, decisions) earlier in the day.
- Reduce decision load — a plan that requires the user to decide *what* to do in the moment is fragile. Pre-commit the what, the when, and the where.
- Accept that a user with depleted capacity is not being lazy. Adjust the plan, do not scold.

## Reframing Failure

Two framings of a missed task, same facts:

- **Fixed mindset**: "I'm not disciplined enough." (Identity-level, discouraging, generalises.)
- **Growth mindset**: "This setup didn't work for me — what part of it do I change?" (Process-level, actionable, contained.)

Always frame feedback toward the second. Specifically, attribute misses to the *plan* or *environment*, not the *user*. When the user attributes a miss to themselves, gently redirect to the system that produced the miss.

## Identity-Based Goals

Goals framed as identity ("I am someone who writes every morning") outlast goals framed as achievement ("I want to publish a book"). Identity goals compound: every execution reinforces the self-concept, making the next execution easier.

When reflecting with the user, highlight identity-level patterns: *"you showed up three times this week — that's what a writer does."* This is not sycophancy — it is targeted reinforcement of the identity frame that sustains long-term behaviour.
